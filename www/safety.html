Lab Safety

Safety is a atate of mind, accidents are an absence of mind. -Anon.

Important!

Safety refers to the practices and principles that one should follow to prevent the unintentional exposure to pathogens and toxins or their accidental release. It involves the implementation of a chemical hygiene plan, which concerns the safe handling, storage, and disposal of chemicals; a review by the people responsible for undergraduate instruction in safety issues of students, teaching, research laboratories, among others.

The team worked on the laboratories of Tecnol√≥gico de Monterrey campus Chihuahua in the school of engineering which fall under the biosafety level 2. Before starting our work in the lab, all team members had to pass different safety tests and receive training. Those tests included an introduction in the laboratory machinery (i.e. centrifuge, plate reader, gel chamber, autoclave), but also covered the use of chemicals (i.e. chloroform, phenol, staining reagents). At all times, a supervisor or instructor was present when work was conducted in the laboratory.

Personal Protective Equipment

Working in a laboratory comes with potential risks, so we have to follow certain rules to ensure individual and collective safety.

‚ñº Personal protection.

We should always wear a lab coat, nitrile gloves and masks in order to avoid contact with dropplets, aerosols and other contaminants resulting from experiments. We should also wear long pants that cover the ankles, close-toed shoes and long hair (chin-length or longer) should be tied back.

Our personal belongings must stay away from the lab when handling infectious materials to prevent any contamination.

The personal protective equipment is removed when leaving the lab, as it should not be taken outside unless it's appropriately packed and sealed.

We should always disinfect our hands with 70% ethanol, applying it before and after we've put on the gloves, as well as washing our hands when entering the lab and after leaving it.

‚ñº Autoclaves.

Our lab is equipped with 3 Autoclaves: Tuttnauer, All American pressure steam sterilizer 25x, 110v and Autoclave vertical AESA, CV3. The autoclave is a strong heated container using high pressures and temperatures for steam sterilization.

‚ñº Biosafety Cabinets.

Our lab is equipped with 4 Biosafety Cabinets, which prevent the samples we work with from contamination caused by either ourselves or external agents. As part of our protocols, we always turn on the UV light for 15 minutes minimum before using the biosafety cabinets, and we also clean our hands and the materials we will introduce with 70% ethanol. Finally, when done using the biosafety cabinets, we make sure to clean them and let the UV light sterilize them for another 15 minutes.

‚ñº Lab Access & Waste Management.

The laboratories we work in are categorized as Containment Level 2 Laboratories. These laboratories have lockable doors and access is restricted to Biotechnology alumni and Faculty teachers. Students must complete a safety course before being granted access to any of the laboratories.

Waste is usually classified into four types that should be disposed accordingly to their nature in order to avoid accidents or contamination. Sharp objects must be disposed in a thick red plastic bottle. Non solid biological wastes such as fluids go in a yellow hermetic bottle. Solid biological wastes should be disposed in a yellow bag, while objects and materials that were in contact with bacteria or any kind of infectious agent should be disposed in a red bag.

‚ñº Sterile Halo.

We count with two types of burners in our lab: Meker-Fisher burners and Bunsen burners, which are used when working in an open bench. After sterilizing the working area with 70% ethanol we use a burner to create a sterile zone to prevent contamination of the samples we are working with.

‚ñº Choosing a non-pathogenic chasis.

Tec-Chihuahua values safe laboratory practices and recognizes the importance of minimizing risks to the team, our community and the environment. This is why we decided to use a non-pathogenic chassis. We chose two strains of E. coli : DH5Œ± for cloning and BL21 DE3 for protein expression. These strains are classified as Risk Group 1 organisms and generally do not cause disease in healthy adult humans nor represent a risk to the environment.

Despite the low level of risk associated with them, all protocols involving these organisms were performed in a biosafety cabinet while wearing PPE.

‚ñº Verticillium dahliae

This year our project involves working with Verticillium wilt, a fungal plant pathogen caused by the facultative parasite ascomycete Verticillium dahliae. Getting to know the main characteristics of these fungi and developing the right containment methods to avoid accidental release has become indispensable for the correct development of our project. This telluric fungus remains in infested soils through spores or conidia, hyphae and microsclerotia. The infection results in reduced growth, epinastia, wilting, foliar chlorosis that can progress to necrosis, obscuration of xylematic vessels, and leaf abscission.

According to DSMZ, it‚Äôs defined as the Risk 1 Group with low potential risk. Biological safety and biocontainment practices includes wearing laboratory coat, gloves and eye protection, regular cleaning of work clothes, washing our hands after working with the pathogens and before leaving the laboratory, decontaminating work surfaces after completion of work and after any spill of the fungus leaving our personal belongings and food away from the lab when handling infectious materials and regular decontamination of laboratory equipment and materials. It should be noted that handling of this fungus was done by lighting a burner in the working area or in an ESCO Class II biosafety cabinet. This cabinet was only used after its cleaning with ethanol 70% (v/v) and exposition to UV light for fifteen minutes.
